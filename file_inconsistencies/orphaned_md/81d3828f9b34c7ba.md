I got this question from a student in the October cohort of [my course](https://maven.com/wes-kao/executive-communication-influence):

> *I‚Äôd love to hear any advice on helping people more senior than me recognize patterns of behavior that are stopping them from getting the results they want.*

> * *Not giving clear guidance during a process--then being disappointed with the results*

> * *Not training new hires--then being disappointed when they take longer to ramp up*

Ah, this is a dilemma.

How can you help a senior leader see they‚Äôre making life harder for themselves and their team?

If you don‚Äôt speak up, you‚Äôre robbing your organization of your [good insights](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/high-performers-share-their-point-of-view).

If you do speak up, you might put your employment and well-being at risk.

This puts you in a tough spot.

Today I want to share some ways you can share feedback with senior leaders, or anyone more powerful than you‚Äîwhile being respectful, helpful, and protecting yourself from their wrath.

#### Giving feedback to someone more powerful than you is a pattern break

Traditionally, the *more* powerful person gives feedback to the *less* powerful person.

So if you want to give feedback to a more senior person, it‚Äôs an **inversion of norms**. This causes a moment of potential chaos, which is why you want to control the energy and channel it in a positive direction.

Feeling nervous about giving your senior leader feedback is a good thing. It means you have survival instincts that help with self-preservation.

Giving a senior leader feedback shouldn‚Äôt be your first option:

* Your first option is to ask yourself, ‚ÄúWhat can I do that‚Äôs within my control to improve this situation?‚Äù
* Your second option is, ‚ÄúCan I live with this? How much does this bother me? Is it worth giving them feedback and what are my chances of success doing it?‚Äù
* Your third, fourth, and fifth option: Thinking more about the above
* Your final option: ‚ÄúI think I want to give this senior leader some feedback.‚Äù

In other words, make sure YOU aren‚Äôt the problem. Make sure you are [managing up](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/15-principles-for-managing-up), asking [clarifying](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/how-to-present-to-your-ceo) [questions](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/question-behind-the-question), [acting like an owner](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/act-like-an-owner), etc to get what you need. It‚Äôs easy to blame a leader, but before you do that, try reflecting on how you could behave differently to improve outcomes for both of you. **It‚Äôs [low-agency](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/weskao_as-a-feedback-receiver-i-need-to-develop-activity-7258123868723138561-OTmF/) to assume other people should change.**

If you do want to give your senior leader feedback, proceed with the appropriate level of caution. You may want to adjust the tactics below based on your specific leader, their idiosyncrasies, [worldview](https://www.weskao.com/blog/understanding-the-worldview-of-a-bureaucrat), your relationship, the level of trust they have in you, etc.

For example, you might realize your relationship or credibility isn‚Äôt strong enough for you to give them feedback‚Äîthat‚Äôs an equally appropriate conclusion.

The only path I‚Äôd advise against: Going in with guns blazing, assuming you have moral high ground simply because you are right.

If you are self-aware and could realistically see your senior leader taking the feedback well, read on.

Here‚Äôs what I do that might be useful for you too.

#### 1. ‚ÄúEven more‚Äù technique

I‚Äôve taught this framework to my clients, and they love it. I‚Äôm going to share it with you.

The framing of ‚Äúeven more‚Äù works so well because you‚Äôre speaking from a place of generosity and giving the benefit of the doubt.

You‚Äôre assuming they‚Äôre doing X‚Äîand that they should do it *even* more. You are assuming they are great--and you want to make them *even* stronger.¬†

**üö´**

> **You**: ‚ÄúWe might need to give guidance to new hires.‚Äù
>
> **Senior leader**: ‚ÄúYou think I don‚Äôt know that I need to give new hires guidance? I obviously gave them guidance. GTFO.‚Äù

**‚úÖ**

> **You**: ‚ÄúWe may need to give even more guidance to new hires.‚Äù
>
> **Senior leader**: ‚ÄúHmm yeah. I gave them some guidance, but that probably wasn‚Äôt enough.‚Äù

**Why this works:**

The ‚Äúbefore‚Äù scenario is binary‚Äîthe senior leader either did or didn‚Äôt give guidance. And if they didn‚Äôt, well, they messed up. They feel threatened that you noticed.

The ‚Äúafter‚Äù is a spectrum: You are generous in assuming that they did give guidance. You‚Äôre suggesting they may want to amp this up even more. This is much easier for them to accept.

This isn‚Äôt simply an issue of semantics or phrasing. This is about positioning to [increase the chances they change their behavior](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/strategy-not-self-expression).

Getting anyone to listen and change their behavior, especially someone more senior? That‚Äôs a huge win.

[The ‚Äúeven more‚Äù technique works with [giving feedback to direct reports too](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/super-specific-feedback). It‚Äôs super versatile.]

#### 2. Use yourself as an example

Talk about yourself so it doesn‚Äôt seem like you‚Äôre pointing fingers. I do this whenever I want to say something the person might find insulting.

> **üö´** ‚ÄúYou should try X.‚Äù
>
> **‚úÖ** ‚ÄúI used to struggle with this, and when I tried X, it really helped.‚Äù¬†

By the way, saying ‚Äúyou should try X‚Äù is fine--there‚Äôs nothing inherently wrong about it. The reason I would avoid it is because of the power dynamic and CONTENT of what I‚Äôm saying.

If there‚Äôs a power dynamic and/or the content of what I‚Äôm saying might be sensitive for the person, that‚Äôs when I share about my own experience.

I‚Äôm not saying YOU are struggling with X, I‚Äôm saying I used to and what I did. And if that lesson applies to what you‚Äôre dealing with, that‚Äôs cool, but no pressure.

#### 3. Adopt diplomatic, curiosity-forward language

Most people are extreme in one of two ways:

1. They think ‚Äúrespecting the person‚Äôs power‚Äù means being 100% supplicant. They do everything the powerful person says, and never push back even when they have reason to. ‚Üí These people are spineless.
2. They share their POV in the [most blunt, low-finesse way](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/tone-and-words-use-accurate-language) without any regard for their recipient‚Äôs [person‚Äôs ego](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/unspoken-power-dynamics-of-calendly). They think, ‚ÄúWell, I‚Äôm right, so I should just say the thing with zero framing.‚Äù ‚Üí These people are insubordinate.

Both are bad. Both lack judgment.

What you want to do is: Share your POV in a way that doesn‚Äôt threaten the senior person, and gets them to listen.

One simple way you can achieve this is by using [diplomatic, curiosity-forward language](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/tone-and-words-use-accurate-language).¬†

These phrases help you ease into the suggestion in a way that feels non-threatening:

* "We might want to try‚Ä¶‚Äù
* ‚ÄúI wonder if...‚Äù
* "Perhaps we could...‚Äù
* ‚ÄúWhat are your thoughts on...‚Äù
* ‚ÄúOne approach might be...‚Äù

You adopt the vibe of a helpful team member who [has your leader‚Äôs best interest in mind](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/oarb-framework-appeal-to-self-interest) and wants to serve your organization.¬†

You are forward-looking. Instead of focusing on what went wrong (which risks making your leader feel defensive), you‚Äôre focused on what to potentially do going forward.

#### 4. Bring data and examples to support your point

If you've been reading this newsletter for a minute, you know I bang on the drum of sharing your logic. I believe most people do not share their rationale enough.

Don‚Äôt just make a high-level, hand-waving statement.

Instead, share examples of what worked well in the past because it helps your feedback land. [Supporting your claims](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/rigorous-thinking) makes you more persuasive and objective--and increases the chances your senior leader will [take your recommendation](https://www.weskao.com/blog/how-to-get-an-enthusiastic-yes).

**Example 1: Guidance during projects**¬†

> **üö´** ‚ÄúWe need to give more feedback during projects.‚Äù
>
> **‚úÖ** ‚ÄúI noticed when we reviewed the design mockups weekly with the client, they loved the final result. For this project, early check-ins could help us make sure we‚Äôre aligned before going too far down a certain direction and could prevent rework.‚Äù

**Example 2: Setting priorities**¬†

> **üö´** ‚ÄúThe team needs clearer priorities.‚Äù
>
> **‚úÖ** ‚ÄúThe team made amazing progress when we all focused on the website update last month. It might help to have one or two clear priorities for the team this month that everyone can rally behind.‚Äù

**Example 3: Training new hires**¬†

> **üö´** ‚ÄúWe should spend more time training new people.‚Äù
>
> **‚úÖ** ‚ÄúWhen we were able to dedicate that first week to training Steve, he got up to speed pretty quickly. The bit of upfront time seemed to have paid off, and taking a similar training approach for our next hire could help them ramp up just as fast. What do you think?‚Äù

**Why this works:**

* Ends with a clear, actionable suggestion
* [Shows the upside](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/oarb-framework-appeal-to-self-interest) of updating their approach
* Keeps it simple, logical, and positive

Personally, I think phrasing a recommendation as a statement reduces cognitive load. You can follow up with an open-ended question, like ‚Äúwhat do you think?‚Äù

Notice how the examples above are [concrete AND concise](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/how-to-be-concise). Avoid long diatribes and [over-explaining](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/start-right-before-you-get-eaten-by-the-bear)‚Äîuse your judgment to share [just enough context](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/how-i-give-the-right-amount-of-context).

And for goodness sake, do *not* have a ‚Äúwell actually‚Ä¶‚Äù vibe when you share logic and data. This is obnoxious and will probably backfire. Notice how in the examples above, I‚Äôm sharing rationale, and doing it in a collaborative way.
